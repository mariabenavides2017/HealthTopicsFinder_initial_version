<p><b>Alzheimer's disease</b> (<b>AD</b>), also known as just <b>Alzheimer's</b>, is a chronic <a href="/wiki/Neurodegeneration" title="Neurodegeneration">neurodegenerative</a> disease that usually starts slowly and gets worse over time.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> It is the cause of 60% to 70% of cases of <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> The most common early symptom is difficulty in remembering recent events (<a href="/wiki/Short-term_memory" title="Short-term memory">short-term memory</a> loss).<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> As the disease advances, symptoms can include <a href="/wiki/Primary_progressive_aphasia" title="Primary progressive aphasia">problems with language</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orientation_(mental)" title="Orientation (mental)">disorientation</a> (including easily getting lost), <a href="/wiki/Mood_swing" title="Mood swing">mood swings</a>, loss of <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">motivation</a>, not managing <a href="/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living" title="Activities of daily living">self care</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Challenging_behaviour" title="Challenging behaviour">behavioural issues</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> As a person's condition declines, they often withdraw from family and society.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> Gradually, bodily functions are lost, ultimately leading to death.<sup id="cite_ref-nihstages_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nihstages-3">[3]</a></sup> Although the speed of progression can vary, the average life expectancy following diagnosis is three to nine years.<sup id="cite_ref-NEJM2010_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM2010-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cause of Alzheimer's disease is poorly understood.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> About 70% of the risk is believed to be <a href="/wiki/Heredity" title="Heredity">genetic</a> with many <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> usually involved.<sup id="cite_ref-Lancet2011_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2011-6">[6]</a></sup> Other risk factors include a history of <a href="/wiki/Head_injury" title="Head injury">head injuries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder" title="Major depressive disorder">depression</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> The disease process is associated with <a href="/wiki/Senile_plaques" title="Senile plaques">plaques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neurofibrillary_tangle" title="Neurofibrillary tangle">tangles</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">brain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lancet2011_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2011-6">[6]</a></sup> A probable diagnosis is based on the history of the illness and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_test" title="Cognitive test">cognitive testing</a> with <a href="/wiki/Medical_imaging" title="Medical imaging">medical imaging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blood_test" title="Blood test">blood tests</a> to rule out other possible causes.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Initial symptoms are often mistaken for normal aging.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> Examination of brain tissue is needed for a definite diagnosis.<sup id="cite_ref-Lancet2011_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2011-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mental_exercise" title="Mental exercise">Mental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_exercise" title="Physical exercise">physical exercise</a>, and avoiding <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> may decrease the risk of AD.<sup id="cite_ref-Lancet2011_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2011-6">[6]</a></sup> There are no medications or supplements that decrease risk.<sup id="cite_ref-prevention1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prevention1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>No treatments stop or reverse its progression, though some may temporarily improve symptoms.<sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> Affected people increasingly rely on others for assistance, often placing a burden on the <a href="/wiki/Caring_for_people_with_dementia" title="Caring for people with dementia">caregiver</a>; the pressures can include social, psychological, physical, and economic elements.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17662119_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17662119-9">[9]</a></sup> Exercise programmes are beneficial with respect to <a href="/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living" title="Activities of daily living">activities of daily living</a> and can potentially improve outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-Forb2013_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forb2013-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Updated version https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25874613 (June 2016)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> Treatment of behavioural problems or <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">psychosis</a> due to dementia with <a href="/wiki/Antipsychotic" title="Antipsychotic">antipsychotics</a> is common but not usually recommended due to there often being little benefit and an increased risk of early death.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, there were approximately 48 million people worldwide with AD.<sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> It most often begins in people over 65&#160;years of age, although 4% to 5% of cases are <a href="/wiki/Early-onset_Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Early-onset Alzheimer's disease">early-onset Alzheimer's</a> which begin before this.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It affects about 6% of people 65 years and older.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2010, dementia resulted in about 486,000 deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-Loz2012_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Loz2012-14">[14]</a></sup> It was first described by, and later named after, German psychiatrist and pathologist <a href="/wiki/Alois_Alzheimer" title="Alois Alzheimer">Alois Alzheimer</a> in 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid9661992_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9661992-15">[15]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a>, AD is one of the most financially costly diseases.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid15685097_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15685097-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid9543467_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9543467-17">[17]</a></sup></p>

<p><b>Alzheimer's disease</b> (<b>AD</b>), also known as just <b>Alzheimer's</b>, is a chronic <a href="/wiki/Neurodegeneration" title="Neurodegeneration">neurodegenerative</a> disease that usually starts slowly and gets worse over time.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> It is the cause of 60% to 70% of cases of <a href="/wiki/Dementia" title="Dementia">dementia</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> The most common early symptom is difficulty in remembering recent events (<a href="/wiki/Short-term_memory" title="Short-term memory">short-term memory</a> loss).<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> As the disease advances, symptoms can include <a href="/wiki/Primary_progressive_aphasia" title="Primary progressive aphasia">problems with language</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orientation_(mental)" title="Orientation (mental)">disorientation</a> (including easily getting lost), <a href="/wiki/Mood_swing" title="Mood swing">mood swings</a>, loss of <a href="/wiki/Motivation" title="Motivation">motivation</a>, not managing <a href="/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living" title="Activities of daily living">self care</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Challenging_behaviour" title="Challenging behaviour">behavioural issues</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> As a person's condition declines, they often withdraw from family and society.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> Gradually, bodily functions are lost, ultimately leading to death.<sup id="cite_ref-nihstages_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nihstages-3">[3]</a></sup> Although the speed of progression can vary, the average life expectancy following diagnosis is three to nine years.<sup id="cite_ref-NEJM2010_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NEJM2010-4">[4]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup></p>
<p>The cause of Alzheimer's disease is poorly understood.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> About 70% of the risk is believed to be <a href="/wiki/Heredity" title="Heredity">genetic</a> with many <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> usually involved.<sup id="cite_ref-Lancet2011_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2011-6">[6]</a></sup> Other risk factors include a history of <a href="/wiki/Head_injury" title="Head injury">head injuries</a>, <a href="/wiki/Major_depressive_disorder" title="Major depressive disorder">depression</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Hypertension" title="Hypertension">hypertension</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> The disease process is associated with <a href="/wiki/Senile_plaques" title="Senile plaques">plaques</a> and <a href="/wiki/Neurofibrillary_tangle" title="Neurofibrillary tangle">tangles</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Human_brain" title="Human brain">brain</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Lancet2011_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2011-6">[6]</a></sup> A probable diagnosis is based on the history of the illness and <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_test" title="Cognitive test">cognitive testing</a> with <a href="/wiki/Medical_imaging" title="Medical imaging">medical imaging</a> and <a href="/wiki/Blood_test" title="Blood test">blood tests</a> to rule out other possible causes.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Initial symptoms are often mistaken for normal aging.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> Examination of brain tissue is needed for a definite diagnosis.<sup id="cite_ref-Lancet2011_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2011-6">[6]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Mental_exercise" title="Mental exercise">Mental</a> and <a href="/wiki/Physical_exercise" title="Physical exercise">physical exercise</a>, and avoiding <a href="/wiki/Obesity" title="Obesity">obesity</a> may decrease the risk of AD.<sup id="cite_ref-Lancet2011_6-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Lancet2011-6">[6]</a></sup> There are no medications or supplements that decrease risk.<sup id="cite_ref-prevention1_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-prevention1-8">[8]</a></sup></p>
<p>No treatments stop or reverse its progression, though some may temporarily improve symptoms.<sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> Affected people increasingly rely on others for assistance, often placing a burden on the <a href="/wiki/Caring_for_people_with_dementia" title="Caring for people with dementia">caregiver</a>; the pressures can include social, psychological, physical, and economic elements.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid17662119_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid17662119-9">[9]</a></sup> Exercise programmes are beneficial with respect to <a href="/wiki/Activities_of_daily_living" title="Activities of daily living">activities of daily living</a> and can potentially improve outcomes.<sup id="cite_ref-Forb2013_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Forb2013-10">[10]</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers"><span title="Updated version https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/25874613 (June 2016)">needs update</span></a></i>]</sup> Treatment of behavioural problems or <a href="/wiki/Psychosis" title="Psychosis">psychosis</a> due to dementia with <a href="/wiki/Antipsychotic" title="Antipsychotic">antipsychotics</a> is common but not usually recommended due to there often being little benefit and an increased risk of early death.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup></p>
<p>In 2015, there were approximately 48 million people worldwide with AD.<sup id="cite_ref-WHO2014_2-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WHO2014-2">[2]</a></sup> It most often begins in people over 65&#160;years of age, although 4% to 5% of cases are <a href="/wiki/Early-onset_Alzheimer%27s_disease" title="Early-onset Alzheimer's disease">early-onset Alzheimer's</a> which begin before this.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> It affects about 6% of people 65 years and older.<sup id="cite_ref-BMJ2009_1-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-BMJ2009-1">[1]</a></sup> In 2010, dementia resulted in about 486,000 deaths.<sup id="cite_ref-Loz2012_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Loz2012-14">[14]</a></sup> It was first described by, and later named after, German psychiatrist and pathologist <a href="/wiki/Alois_Alzheimer" title="Alois Alzheimer">Alois Alzheimer</a> in 1906.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid9661992_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9661992-15">[15]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/Developed_country" title="Developed country">developed countries</a>, AD is one of the most financially costly diseases.<sup id="cite_ref-pmid15685097_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid15685097-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-pmid9543467_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pmid9543467-17">[17]</a></sup></p>
